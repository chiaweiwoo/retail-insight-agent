## 2. Findings

### Sales performance
- **Current sales:** $270.8 — well above the trailing 7d avg of $184.48 (+47%) and the same-weekday-4wk avg of $209.41 (+29%).
- The prior day (Sat 5/4) also showed elevated sales ($219.4), suggesting a multi-day lift possibly tied to the labor day holiday period.
- Sales on 5/5 are the **highest** in the 8-day window shown.

### Stockout / availability picture
| Metric | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Avg stockout hours** | 4.44 hrs | Moderately elevated — roughly 18.5% of the trading day had some stockout condition. |
| **Stockout product rate** | 56.8% | More than half of products experienced at least one stockout event during the day. |
| **Severe stockout rate** | 30.3% | Nearly a third of products had a deep or prolonged stockout. |
| **Full stockout rate** | 1.9% | Very few products were completely out of stock all day. |
| **Peak hourly stockout rate** | 59.3% | At the worst hour, ~59% of products were unavailable. |

### Assessment
- **Sales are UP sharply despite high stockout pressure.** This is unusual — typically, broad stockouts constrain sales rather than coincide with them spiking. This suggests that **demand was exceptionally strong** (labor day holiday buying) and likely overwhelmed shelf availability, *not* that stockouts caused the sales move.
- The **cause-vs-consequence ambiguity is important here:** Are stockouts *causing* lost sales, or is the stockout pattern a *consequence* of unexpectedly high demand pulling inventory down faster? Given that sales are well above any recent baseline, the latter is more plausible. The high stockout product rate (56.8%) alongside rising sales points to **demand outstripping replenishment velocity** rather than a supply-side failure dragging sales down.
- That said, the ~4.4 hours of average stockout duration means there **was foregone upside** — if inventory had been deeper, sales might have been even higher. The severe stockout rate of 30% is a red flag for any items that could not be sold during demand peaks.

---

## 3. Caveats

- **Directionality is ambiguous.** High stockout metrics and high sales coexist. Without item-level depletion curves, we cannot cleanly separate demand shock from stocking failure. The data better supports a **demand-driven explanation** (holiday period surge) with secondary inventory pressure.
- **"Labor day period"** may distort baselines. The 4-week same-weekday average ($209.41) likely doesn't include prior labor day periods, so the "baseline" may understate expected demand.
- **No forward data** — we cannot yet see if the stockouts led to disappointed customers on subsequent days (carryover demand loss), which is a common hidden cost of high-severity stockouts.
- The **full stockout rate is low (1.9%)**, meaning the store remained operational across nearly all SKUs; the issue is intermittent availability, not complete category shutdowns.
