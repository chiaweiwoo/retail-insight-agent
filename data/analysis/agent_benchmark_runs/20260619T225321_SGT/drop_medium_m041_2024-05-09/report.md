# RCA Report — Store m041, 2024-05-09

## 1. Trigger

Signal DROP: sales of $56.67, **-33.0%** vs trailing 7-day average ($84.64) and **-15.8%** vs same-weekday 4-week average ($67.30). Signal is valid and classified as a notable decline.

## 2. Likely Drivers

1.  **Supply-side failure (stockouts) — primary driver.** Severe, widespread product unavailability (38% of products affected, 61% peak hourly rate, ~3-hour average duration) is the most plausible explanation for the magnitude and shape of the drop.
2.  **Post-holiday demand fade — contributing factor.** The Labor Day period (May 1–5) temporarily inflated sales, and May 9 sits at the end of a 4-day softening trend post-holiday. However, the drop is *deeper* than a typical mean reversion would explain, and peer stores did not show a similar decline.
3.  **Localized issue (not chain-wide).** The store ranked 5th out of 5 in its tier and 10th out of 15 overall. Tier average was $73.12 vs $56.67 (22.5% gap), confirming the problem is specific to m041, not a market-wide event.

**Discounting/pricing — ruled out as a cause.** Moderate breadth (46% discounted), shallow depth (7.4% average discount), no deep discounting. Not a pricing-driven decline.

**Weather/calendar — ruled out.** Mild conditions, no disruption, normal Thursday.

**Key inference:** The stockout severity (~38% of items affected, 3-hour average gap) is consistent with a ~$28 (33%) sales shortfall. On a day when overall sales are *down*, broad stockouts are more parsimoniously explained by a supply-side failure (replenishment/ordering/delivery issue) than by demand-side sell-through spikes.

## 4. Caveats

- **Cause vs. consequence ambiguity remains.** Isolated hot items stocking out from demand *could* occur, but they would not explain a 38% product-level stockout rate on a down-sales day. The inventory analyst judges supply-side failure the more likely read.
- **Same-weekday 4-week baseline ($67.30) includes Labor Day (May 2)**, which may inflate it slightly. Even so, current sales ($56.67) undercut it significantly.
- **Trailing 7-day avg ($84.64) includes elevated Labor Day weekend sales (May 4–5).** This partly inflates the -33% comparison but does not explain the peer underperformance or the same-weekday gap.
- **No category-level or item-level inventory data** was available. A deeper drill could distinguish systemic failure vs. single-category impact.
- **Foot traffic data** was not available. Stockouts could reduce conversion without affecting traffic, but a traffic drop from a local event or competitor action could also reduce sales (though weather and calendar rule out obvious disruption).
- **No staffing or operational data.** A delivery miss, truck delay, or labor shortage could explain the empty shelves.

## 5. Suggested Next Checks

1.  **Verify supply/replenishment incident.** Check delivery logs, truck arrival times, and order quantities for May 8–9. Look for missed or partial deliveries.
2.  **Category-level stockout drill.** Identify which departments had the highest stockout rates. If concentrated in grocery/perishables, suspect a cold-chain or delivery failure. If broad-based across dry goods, suspect a systemic ordering or allocation problem.
3.  **Hourly sales pattern.** Compare hourly sales on May 9 vs. May 8 and vs. typical Thursday. If sales were already low at store open, stockouts likely predated the day. If sales started normal then collapsed midday, a late stockout event (e.g., afternoon truck missed) is more likely.
4.  **Friday May 10 monitoring.** Check if sales rebounded. A sharp recovery would support a single-day stockout event. Continued weakness would suggest a more persistent issue (e.g., ongoing replenishment failure or traffic decline).
