The stockout picture is severe enough to be a plausible primary driver of the sales miss:

  1. Availability pressure was extreme. A 61% peak stockout rate means customers at that hour found more than half the shelf empty. Even a customer arriving earlier or later experienced roughly 3 hours of average unavailability, which suppresses basket size and conversion.
  2. The magnitude fits. A ~$28 gap vs. trailing avg (~33% decline) is consistent with a store where more than a third of products had gaps. Losing sales on 38% of items, even partially, would produce this order of drop.
  3. No obvious demand-side explanation. The day is a normal Thursday with no holiday periods; prior-week Labor Day (May 1–5) has passed. The same-weekday baseline from prior weeks is $67.30, so even that "normal" benchmark was undercut by ~16%.

Causality caveat: It is possible that some stockouts are consequences of demand spikes (e.g., a product sold out fast because it was unusually popular). However, the overall sales level is down, not up — so demand spikes are unlikely to explain a broad, multi-category stockout pattern. The more parsimonious read is supply-side failure: ordering/delivery/replenishment issues left the store understocked, which materially suppressed sales.

3. Caveats

  • Cause vs. consequence ambiguity. While stockouts on a down-sales day are most likely supply-driven, isolated, fast-moving items could have stocked out because they sold well (but this would not explain the broad ~38% rate).
  • No category/item-level data in the current output. A deeper drill into which departments or SKUs were affected would help distinguish between a systemic replenishment failure vs. a few key categories dragging down totals.
  • Recent history includes Labor Day period (May 1–5). The trailing 7d avg includes some holiday-elevated days (May 4: $94.50, May 5: $116.48), which may slightly inflate the baseline. However, the same-weekday 4w avg ($67.30) also exceeds current sales ($56.67), confirming a real shortfall.
  • No competitor or local disruption data. External factors (e.g., a nearby event, road closure, competitor promotion) could suppress traffic, which would also reduce sales but might not be inventory-driven.

Bottom line: Stockout conditions on 2024-05-09 at store m041 were severe — ~38% of products affected, 3-hour average duration, a 61% peak — and this very likely explains a material portion of the ~$28 (33%) sales drop below the trailing average. Inventory failure should be the lead hypothesis for further RCA.
