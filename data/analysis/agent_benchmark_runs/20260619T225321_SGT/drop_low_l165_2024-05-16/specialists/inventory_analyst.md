## Inventory Specialist Memo — Store l165, 2024-05-16

### 1. Scope

Assessment of stockout conditions and availability pressure for store **l165** on **2024-05-16 (Thursday, normal weekday)**.

### 2. Findings

**Sales Performance**
- Current sales: **$25.38**
- Trailing 7-day avg: **$37.17** → sales are **~32% below** recent average
- Same-weekday 4-week avg (Thursdays): **$28.05** → sales are only **~10% below** the prior Thursday baseline
- Prior day (Wed 5/15): **$33.50** → sales dropped **24% day-over-day**

**Stockout Severity — Elevated**

| Metric | Value | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Avg stockout hours per product | **1.86 hrs** | Moderate duration |
| Products touched by stockout (%) | **27.3%** | **High** — over a quarter of SKUs |
| Severe stockouts (%) | **11.4%** | **Notable** — 1-in-9 products had meaningful gaps |
| Full-stockout products (%) | **2.3%** | Low (likely perishable or low-velocity lines) |
| Peak hourly stockout rate | **36.4%** | **Very high** — availability collapsed at worst hour |

**Interpretation**

Stockout conditions were **materially worse than typical**. Key pressure points:
- **27% product-level stockout rate** means availability problems were widespread, not isolated to a handful of items.
- The **severe stockout rate (11.4%)** suggests that for ~1-in-9 products, the outage lasted long enough to meaningfully suppress sales.
- The **peak hourly rate of 36.4%** indicates a period where over a third of the assortment was unavailable — likely a replenishment gap (delayed truck, receiving lag, or a busy period that exhausted inventory).

**Cause vs. Consequence Ambiguity**

- It is **plausible that stockouts caused a meaningful portion of the sales shortfall**. The ~$11.79 gap vs. the trailing 7d avg ($25.38 vs $37.17) could be partially explained by frustrated customers unable to find key items.
- However, the **same-weekday baseline (Thursdays) is only $28.05**, meaning the day was only ~$2.67 below the typical Thursday. This tempers the stockout narrative — some of the "gap" vs. the trailing average is likely just normal day-of-week variation.
- It is also possible that **lower-than-expected traffic/demand contributed to the stockouts** (e.g., if over-ordering led to allocation that depleted shelf inventory but left backroom stock — though the data doesn't distinguish shelf vs. warehouse availability).
- A **causal link runs both ways**: stockouts drive sales down, but lower sales can also leave more inventory on hand, which is the *opposite* of a stockout. So the direction here leans toward *stockouts causing sales loss*, not the reverse.

### 3. Caveats

- **No SKU-level detail**: We know ~27% of products were affected, but not which ones. If the stockouts hit high-velocity, high-margin items, the revenue impact would be amplified compared to low-velocity SKUs.
- **Hourly pattern unknown**: A 36.4% peak stockout rate is alarming, but without knowing *when* it occurred (opening, lunch rush, evening), we can't tie it cleanly to the $25.38 daily total.
- **Thursday baseline is noisy**: The same-weekday average ($28.05) includes a Labor Day period Thursday (5/2: $27.10) which may suppress the comparison. The more "normal" Thursday (5/9: $26.50) is actually very close to current sales ($25.38), suggesting the stockouts may only explain ~$1-2 of the gap.
- **No POS vs. inventory reconciliation**: We cannot determine if the stockouts stemmed from ordering issues, delivery delays, or unexpected demand spikes on specific items.
- **Recommendation**: Cross-reference with supply chain/logistics data for 5/16 to see if a delivery was missed or delayed, and check whether the 11.4% severe-stockout SKUs overlap with the store's top-20 revenue items.
