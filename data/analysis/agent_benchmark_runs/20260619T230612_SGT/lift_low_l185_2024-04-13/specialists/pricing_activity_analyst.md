## 2. Findings

- **Price level:** The average discount factor (~92% of full price, i.e. an ~8% discount) is stable and consistent with recent days. There is no deep discounting (0% deep discount rate).
- **Discount breadth:** On Apr 13, 46.3% of products were discounted — *lower* than the prior two days (53.7% and 51.2%). This is not a breadth-driven spike.

### Promotional activity
| Metric | Apr 13 | Apr 12 | Apr 11 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Activity product rate (% of SKUs) | 36.6% | 36.6% | 36.6% |
| Activity sales share (% of revenue) | **48.4%** | 38.1% | 33.2% |

- The **share of sales coming from promotion-tagged items jumped sharply** to 48.4%, up from 38.1% yesterday and 33.2% the day before.
- Notably, the *product rate* for activity items is flat at 36.6% — the same SKU set is likely still promoted. The revenue share increase indicates that **customers selected promoted items at a much higher rate**, rather than the store adding new promotional mechanics.

### Key interpretation
- **Pricing itself (discount depth) is not the driver** — average discount holds steady at ~92% with no fire-sale pricing.
- **Discount breadth actually contracted** day-over-day, so this is not a "more items on sale" story.
- **The promotional activity share rose by ~10 pp** while the promotional SKU count stayed constant. This points to **improved promotional mix and/or customer response** — shoppers bought heavily from the promoted assortment on this Saturday, boosting basket value.

The sales spike is best explained by **strong promotional uptake** (customers gravitating toward promoted SKUs) on a weekend day, layered on top of a stable, moderate discount structure. No price-cutting escalation is evident.

## 3. Caveats

- Average discount is a global store-level metric; individual item-level mixed shifts (e.g., customers buying higher-priced promoted goods) could influence the total revenue without changing the discount factor.
- The 14-day history window does not provide a full same-weekday-of-previous-month baseline (returned null). The comparison to Qingming-period Saturday (Apr 06, $41.50) is imperfect due to holiday context.
- "Activity" category definition is not specified — it may capture temporary price reductions, featured items, or other in-store mechanics. The observed jump in activity sales share could also reflect broader external demand (e.g., local event, weather) rather than a promotional cause.
- The memo focuses only on store l185 on this single date; attribution of causality would benefit from cross-store or category-level benchmarks.
