The dominant driver is a holiday calendar effect (Labor Day period, May 1-5), layered with a store-specific outperformance beyond peers. Key contributors:

  1. Labor Day period demand surge — Sales ramped from May 1 onward, peaking on May 5. Holiday promotional activity (~7% average discount on 47% of products) created a broad "value signal" without deep discounting.

  2. Strong promotional execution — 36% of sales came from actively promoted items, with no clearance-level discounts. This suggests disciplined, margin-aware holiday pricing that still drove incremental volume.

  3. Store-specific local tailwind — h235 ranked #1 overall out of 15 stores and 12% above its Tier H average ($241.09). This outperformance beyond the chain-wide holiday lift points to a local factor (weather, catchment demographics, or management execution).

  4. Extreme availability pressure did not cap revenue — Despite 56.8% of products experiencing stockouts and a peak of 59% hourly unavailability, sales still hit $270.80. This indicates demand was so strong that constrained availability still produced record revenue, though likely with substitution effects.

4. Caveats

  • Stockout vs demand direction ambiguous — The high stockout rate (56.8%) could either mean demand was so strong it overwhelmed inventory (likely), or that stockouts suppressed what would have been even higher sales. Cannot quantify lost revenue without item-level sales-vs-stockout overlap data.

  • Baseline contamination — The 7-day trailing average ($184.48) includes the May 1-5 holiday ramp. A pre-holiday 4-week baseline would likely show an even larger actual lift. The same-weekday-4wk average ($209.41) partially compensates but still includes Apr 21 ($250.82, an anomalously strong Sunday).

  • Weather attribution is speculative — 2.65mm rain could drive people to this store or keep them home. No "similar weather, non-holiday" benchmark exists for this store.

  • "Labor Day period" definition — May 1 is Labor Day in many countries, but the actual holiday timing and its impact on this specific store's catchment is not verified locally. Different markets may observe it differently.

  • Price elasticities unknown — Cannot isolate whether the 7.2% average discount drove volume, or whether demand was inelastic and the discount merely transferred margin. No unit volume data was available.

  • No profit/margin context — Revenue was high, but the cost of promotions and stockout-driven substitution effects on margin are unknown.

5. Suggested Next Checks

  1. Item-level stockout vs. sales overlap — Determine exactly which products stocked out and whether customers substituted to higher- or lower-margin items. This would quantify lost revenue vs. substitution gains.

  2. Pre-holiday baseline — Recalculate lift using a 4-week pre-holiday (non-holiday) average (e.g., Apr 1-28) to get a cleaner holiday effect magnitude.

  3. Tier H performance decomposition — Investigate why h235 outperformed its Tier H peers by 12%. Possible areas: local marketing, manager discretion on promotions, or catchment demographics that respond differently to damp weather.

  4. Replenishment cycle review — The 4.4-hour average stockout duration suggests inventory planning did not anticipate the demand surge. Review whether reorder points or delivery schedules were adequate for holiday weekends.

  5. Weather-sales correlation — Check whether this store historically sees above-average traffic on damp Sundays vs. clear Sundays, to validate the weather hypothesis.

  6. Unit volume vs. revenue analysis — Check if the $270.80 revenue was achieved with normal or reduced unit volume (suggesting price/mix shift due to stockout substitution).
