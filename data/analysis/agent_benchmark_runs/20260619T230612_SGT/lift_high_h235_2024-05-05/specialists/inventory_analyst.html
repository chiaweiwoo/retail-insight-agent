1. Scope

Store: h235
Date: 2024-05-05 (Sunday, during Labor Day period)
Focus: Stockout severity, availability pressure, and potential inventory contribution to sales movement.

2. Findings

Sales Context

  • Current sales: $270.8 — 47% above the trailing 7-day average ($184.5) and 29% above the same-weekday-4-week average ($209.4).
  • Sales have been elevated across the Labor Day period (May 1–5), peaking on this Sunday. The prior day (Saturday May 4) was also strong at $219.4.
  • This is not a routine level — demand is clearly spiking during the holiday period.

Stockout & Availability Assessment

Metric Value Interpretation

Stockout product rate 56.8% Very high — over half of products experienced a stockout event
Severe stockout rate 30.3% Nearly a third of products had severe stockout conditions

Key Diagnosis

  • Availability was heavily pressured. The stockout product rate (56.8%) is extremely elevated. This store was operating with more than half its assortment unavailable at some point during the day.
  • Despite this, sales still hit a high. This likely means:
  • Demand was so strong that even constrained availability couldn't cap revenue.
  • Customers may have substituted to available items (higher-value or different mix), possibly inflating dollar sales.
  • The severe stockout rate (30%) suggests a meaningful portion of demand may have been lost — actual latent demand could have been even higher.
  • Peak hourly stockout ~59% confirms the issue was broad and not limited to a single category or aisle.

Cause vs. Consequence Ambiguity

  • Did stockouts cause suppressed sales or did demand spike cause stockouts? Likely demand spike (Labor Day weekend) caused the stockouts. The store saw a multi-day demand surge, inventory depleted faster than replenishment could keep up, and the stockout metrics reflect a store that was selling through faster than planned.
  • However, the stockouts themselves may have capped upside. If not for the availability gaps, sales might have been even higher than $270.8.
  • The 4.4-hour average stockout duration suggests that replenishment cycles existed but were too slow relative to the demand rate — a classic holiday demand-inventory mismatch.

3. Caveats

  • This is a correlational assessment. Without item-level sales vs. stockout overlap data, I cannot quantify exactly how much revenue was lost to stockouts vs. how much was merely demand-driven.
  • The elevated sales despite high stockout rates could partly reflect price/mix shifts (e.g., customers buying premium substitutes). Dollar sales alone can mask unit volume declines.
  • The baseline period (7 trailing days) includes the start of the Labor Day period itself, so "normal" may be slightly inflated relative to a true non-holiday baseline.
  • No supply chain data (e.g., delivery schedules, warehouse backorders) was reviewed — the root cause of stockouts (under-ordering vs. late truck vs. unexpected demand) is inferred, not confirmed.
  • "Labor day period" in the holiday label may refer to May 1 Labor Day (observed in many countries) — actual demand patterns would vary by market.
