RCA Report: Store m041 – 2024-05-09

1. Trigger

Signal drop alert: sales of $56.67 represent a -33.0% decline vs trailing 7-day average ($84.64), crossing the -20% threshold. Day-over-day decline from $75.30 (Wed) to $56.67 (Thu) is -24.7%.

2. Likely Drivers

Primary driver: Post-holiday inventory depletion causing severe stockouts

The Labor Day holiday period (May 1–5) drove elevated sales (peaked at $116.48 on May 5), drawing down inventory faster than replenishment cycles could recover. By May 9, the store experienced:

  • 37.7% of SKUs with at least one stockout event
  • 60.9% peak-hour stockout rate — 6 out of 10 products unavailable simultaneously
  • 15.9% severe stockout rate (~1 in 6 SKUs heavily impacted)

This is the strongest candidate explanation: customers encountered empty shelves, resulting in lost sales or substitution to lower-value items.

Secondary factor: Genuine demand decline from holiday normalization

Post-holiday Monday–Wednesday showed a gradual decline ($83.51 → $79.94 → $75.30), which is expected. However, the additional -24.7% drop on Thursday exceeds normal reversion. The same-weekday baseline ($67.30) was also 19% above actuals, indicating some demand loss beyond inventory issues.

Minor factor: Light precipitation (0.47 inches)

Weather was mild (21.5°C, light rain) — likely a mild foot-traffic suppressant but cannot explain the magnitude of underperformance versus peers.

4. Caveats

  • No product-level data — cannot determine whether stockouts were concentrated in high-velocity categories (which would explain the sales impact) or spread evenly.
  • No shipment/inbound data — cannot distinguish between ordering misses, warehouse shorts, or delayed deliveries as the root cause of stockouts.
  • Ambiguous causation: Low foot traffic could reduce sales AND reduce shelf restocking activity, inflating stockout metrics. The peak-hour rate of 60.9% makes pure demand-driven explanation unlikely, but cannot be fully ruled out.
  • Trailing 7-day avg ($84.64) is inflated by Labor Day holiday (May 1–5). The -33% headline is partly a holiday normalization effect; the more conservative -15.8% vs same-weekday baseline is the cleaner comparison.
  • No competitor or local disruption data — a nearby competitor opening/promotion or local event (road closure, power outage) could explain the peer underperformance.

5. Suggested Next Checks

  1. Product-level stockout drilldown — Identify which SKUs/categories were worst-affected. If high-velocity, high-margin SKUs were out of stock, the sales impact is directly explained.

  2. Inbound shipment reconciliation — Check if scheduled deliveries for May 8–9 arrived on time. A missed or short shipment would confirm the ordering/replenishment hypothesis.

  3. Labor Day inventory drawdown analysis — Compare inventory levels before (Apr 30) and after (May 6) the holiday period to see if buffers were depleted.

  4. Foot traffic data — If available, compare store traffic to tier peers. A traffic drop alone would suggest demand shock; normal traffic with low conversion would support the stockout theory.

  5. Store manager interview — Inquire about staffing levels, restocking schedules, and whether any incidents (e.g., delivery disruption, system outage) occurred on May 8–9.
