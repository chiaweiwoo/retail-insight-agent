### Key Interpretation

**Stockout pressure is very high**, but it is running *with* the sales spike, not against it. The pattern suggests:

- High demand is depleting inventory faster than usual — the stockouts are likely a **consequence** of elevated sales, not the cause.
- Availability problems would typically *depress* sales, yet sales are at a notable peak. This supports a demand-driven cause (not an inventory-driven one).
- The severe stockout rate (27.5%) and average 4.33 hours out-of-stock per SKU indicate real availability friction, but it has not (yet) capped top-line sales.

**Comparative note:** The prior Sunday (2024-05-05, $116.48) was within the Labor Day period and likely had similar or higher inventory levels pre-shift. The further jump to $146.4 on 2024-05-12 may reflect residual holiday demand or a local event/promotion.

## 3. Caveats

- **Cause vs. consequence ambiguity:** Stockouts are correlated with high sales, but the direction is almost certainly sales → stockouts on this date, not the reverse. Without replenishment timing data, I cannot confirm whether pre-existing low inventory contributed to the sales pattern.
- **Item-level data:** The aggregate metrics show broad availability pressure, but I have not drilled into specific categories or top sellers. A few key high-margin SKUs being out of stock could have a disproportionate financial impact even if total sales remained high.
- **Holiday context:** The Labor Day period (May 1–5) showed elevated sales. May 12 being a Sunday one week later could still be experiencing a demand tail, though the holiday label shows "weekend" not "labor_day_period."
- **No external demand signals:** A local event, promotion, or competitor closure could explain the demand surge without any inventory cause whatsoever.
- **Replenishment timing:** 4.33 average stockout hours suggests the store recovers stock during the day to some degree, but I cannot assess whether restocking cadence was normal or delayed.

**Bottom line:** Inventory issues are **not the likely driver** of this sales move. The evidence points to a demand surge that strained availability. If anything, stockouts may have *limited* what could have been an even higher sales day.
