2. Likely Drivers

  1. Mother's Day demand surge (primary driver) – May 12, 2024 was the second Sunday of May. The broad-based lift across both promoted and non-promoted items (~60% non-promoted sales), moderate discounting (~10% avg), and the seasonal timing all point to a gift-giving holiday effect rather than a pure price promotion.

  2. Demand-driven stockout strain (consequence, not cause) – Stockouts are elevated (avg 4.33 hrs, 55.1% product rate) but running with the sales spike, not against it. This is consistent with high demand depleting inventory faster than normal, not pre-existing availability issues suppressing sales.

  3. Local / store-specific factor (possible additional driver) – m041 ranked #1 in its tier (+19.5% vs tier avg) and #6 chain-wide. The spike is idiosyncratic; peers averaged only $122.52. Weather and calendar alone do not explain the gap vs. tier peers.

Inferred chain of events: Mother's Day generated elevated demand at the store level. This demand surge caused stockouts as inventory was depleted faster than normal. Modest promotional activity (~46% products promoted) supported but did not drive the magnitude of the spike.

4. Caveats

  • Mother's Day attribution is inferred from calendar pattern, not explicitly labeled. The context analyst notes the holiday label shows "weekend" not "mothers_day." This should be validated against the retailer's event calendar.
  • Category-level variation is hidden. Store-wide averages may obscure important sub-trends (e.g., jewelry, electronics, or floral driving the spike vs. lower-ticket promoted items).
  • Stockout directionality is inferred, not proven. Without replenishment timing data, a pre-existing low-inventory condition cannot be fully ruled out, though the high concurrent sales make sales→stockouts the more plausible direction.
  • No data on local events, competitor closures, or staffing changes at store m041 that could explain the idiosyncratic outperformance vs. peers.
  • Sales vs. profit unknown. High sales with elevated stockouts (esp. 27.5% severe rate) may indicate lost margin on key SKUs, even if top-line was strong.
  • No data on online vs. in-store channel split or whether click-and-collect contributed.

5. Suggested Next Checks

  1. Validate Mother's Day attribution – Check retailer event calendar for store m041 on May 12. Confirm holiday designation and whether targeted promotions or displays were active.

  2. Item-level drill-down – Identify which categories/SKUs drove the highest dollar contribution. Check if high-margin items (jewelry, electronics, floral) were disproportionately affected by stockouts.

  3. Replenishment timing – Pull restock logs for May 12 to confirm whether stockouts followed demand or preceded it (e.g., delayed truck arrival).

  4. Local event / competitor check – Investigate if a nearby competitor closed, a local event drew traffic, or the store ran a specific marketing campaign (e.g., radio, social media) on May 11-12.

  5. Peer comparison drill-down – Compare m041's category mix vs. tier peers on May 12. If the gap is concentrated in gift categories, this strengthens the holiday-seasonal hypothesis.

  6. Profit impact assessment – Estimate lost margin from stockouts on high-value SKUs vs. incremental revenue from the spike. Determine if the surge was net beneficial.

  7. Category-level pricing analysis – Check if certain categories had deeper discounts than the store-wide avg (~10%) to identify if any sub-promotion was exceptionally aggressive.
