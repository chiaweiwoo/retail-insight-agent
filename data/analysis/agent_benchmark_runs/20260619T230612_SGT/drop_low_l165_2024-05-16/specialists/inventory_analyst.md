## 2. Findings

### Sales Performance
- **Actual sales:** $25.38

### Stockout Position
| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| **Avg stockout hours per product** | 1.86 hrs |
| **% of products with any stockout** | **27.3%** (alarming) |
| **% of products with severe stockout (>4hr)** | **11.4%** (high) |

**Key observation:** More than one in four products experienced at least some stockout on this day, and over one in ten had severe stockouts exceeding four hours. The peak hour saw 36% of all products unavailable simultaneously — a significant availability failure.

### Cause vs. Consequence Ambiguity
- **Stockouts likely contributed to the sales shortfall.** With 27% of products facing stockouts and a 36% peak unavailability rate, the store was materially under-shelved. Customers would have found empty facings, particularly in the peak hours, directly suppressing conversion and basket size.
- **However, causality runs both ways.** Lower-than-expected foot traffic could mean less shelf-scanning and fewer replenishment triggers, but the severity (11% severe stockouts) suggests a structural availability problem (under-ordering, DC misses, or poor shelf management) rather than just demand-side weakness.
- The Thursday same-weekday baseline ($28.05) is already low, indicating this store has chronic Thursday softness. The gap to $25.38 is modest but still material.

### Availability Pressure Assessment
**HIGH.** The stockout product rate of 27.3% is well above what would be considered healthy (<5% is typical target). The severe rate of 11.4% indicates that some categories were bare for hours. This likely explains at least a portion of the ~$8–12 gap to the trailing 7d average.

## 3. Caveats

- **Product-level data not available.** We see aggregate stockout rates but cannot determine which specific SKUs or categories were affected. High-velocity / high-margin items being out would matter more than slow-movers.
- **No replenishment log data.** We cannot distinguish between "ordered but not delivered" vs. "ordered, received, but not worked to shelf" vs. "not ordered."
- **Sales baseline noise.** The trailing 7d average includes Sunday 05/12 ($57.20 — a weekend), pulling the average upward. A better comparison is the Thursday-specific baselines, where the gap is smaller (~$2.67 below 4w avg).
- **Holiday context.** The prior Thursday (05/02) fell in a "labor day period" which may have altered demand patterns — the 4-week same-weekday average includes that anomalous week.
- **Stockout-sales direction.** Without timestamp-level sales and stockout correlation, we cannot definitively prove stockouts caused the miss vs. being a result of it. The high severe rate makes a causal role very likely, but some degree of simultaneity bias exists.
